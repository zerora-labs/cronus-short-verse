# CronusShortVerse Rules

## 1. Celestial Body Classification

Every narrative entity (character, event, location) is assigned one of five types based on its **narrative mass and lifespan**.

| Type           | Mass                    | Lifespan                | Example                                        |
| -------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **Star**       | Very High               | Entire series           | Main protagonist                               |
| **Planet**     | High                    | Most of the series      | Recurring supporting character                 |
| **Comet**      | Medium                  | 1–3 episodes            | Guest character, temporary plot device         |
| **Meteor**     | Low                     | Single episode or scene | One‑off rumor, flashback                       |
| **Black Hole** | Variable (but singular) | Permanent mystery       | Secret, unresolved trauma, forbidden knowledge |

**Rule 1.1**: A star can have multiple planets orbiting it. Planets can have moons (minor characters) but moons are not tracked in the core model.

**Rule 1.2**: A comet can become a planet if it appears in more than 3 episodes. Its mass increases accordingly.

**Rule 1.3**: A meteor leaves no permanent orbit. It may affect the trajectory of planets or stars temporarily.

**Rule 1.4**: A black hole is never fully revealed. Its effects are felt (characters getting pulled in, events spiraling), but its nature remains unknown.

## 2. Gravitational Interactions

- **Attraction**: Two characters with strong narrative ties (e.g., hero and villain) exert gravitational pull on each other.
- **Repulsion**: Characters with opposing goals push each other away.
- **Orbit**: A character who consistently follows another is in orbit.
- **Collision**: When two stars directly conflict, the outcome is a major plot point.

**Rule 2.1**: The force of attraction is proportional to the product of their masses divided by the square of their narrative distance.

**Rule 2.2**: A black hole can absorb any celestial body that gets too close. The absorbed body is removed from the active universe (e.g., a character leaves or dies).

## 3. Time and Cycles

- **Episode**: The smallest time unit. Each episode is approximately 2 minutes.
- **Arc**: A collection of episodes (usually 10–20) that forms a complete story cycle.
- **Season**: Multiple arcs that share an overarching theme.
- **Cycle Reset**: At the end of an arc, some orbits may reset (characters return to their default positions) while others persist.

**Rule 3.1**: Cliffhangers at the end of an episode increase narrative velocity for the next episode.

**Rule 3.2**: Time loops are allowed. When a loop occurs, the same characters repeat actions but with small orbital shifts each cycle.

## 4. Noise and Signal

Not every event in a short drama is narratively significant. The **noise filter** distinguishes:

- **Signal**: Events that change character states, relationships, or plot direction.
- **Noise**: Filler content, background activities, or events that do not affect the main narrative.

**Rule 4.1**: Only signal events are recorded in the universe history. Noise events are discarded.

**Rule 4.2**: A meteor is typically noise unless it triggers a signal event.

## 5. Extensibility

Creators can define new celestial body types (e.g., Nebula for mysterious zones, Pulsar for recurring surprise elements) as long as they respect the gravitational model.
